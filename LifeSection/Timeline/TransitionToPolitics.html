The rise of the democracy in Ethiopia

Transition

To

Politics

In the 1990s, Ethiopia underwent a significant political

transformation following the fall of the Derg regime

and the rise of the Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front (EPRDF).


It was during this period of transition that Professor Beyene Petros entered politics,

driven by a desire to contribute to the country’s democratization process and to address the systemic issues he had observed throughout his career.


His decision to enter politics was deeply rooted in his commitment to addressing Ethiopia’s systemic challenges and improving the lives of its citizens.


As a scientist, he had spent years studying Ethiopia’s public health crises and advocating for evidence-based interventions.


However, he came to realize that lasting change required political solutions to the country’s entrenched problems of poverty, inequality, and authoritarian governance.


Beyene’s entry into politics must be understood within the broader historical and political context of Ethiopia during the 1990s.

This was a period of profound transformation.

The fall of the Derg regime in 1991,

following years of brutal military rule,

marked the end of a

dark chapter in

Ethiopian history.

The Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front (EPRDF), a coalition of rebel groups, took power and established a transitional government, promising democratization, federalism, and political pluralism.

While many Ethiopians welcomed the departure of the Derg

and hoped for a more democratic future,

cracks soon began to appear in the EPRDF’s governance.

Motivations for

Entering

Politics

Beyene Petros’s transition to politics was not a sudden or impulsive decision but the culmination of years of observation and reflection.

As a scientist and educator, he had witnessed firsthand the devastating impact of Ethiopia’s socio-political challenges on the country’s health, education, and development. His deep commitment to public health and social justice became the driving forces behind his political ambitions.

Between 1989 To 1991

Beyene was a firm believer in democracy, human rights, and the rule of law.

He viewed political participation as a moral obligation for educated Ethiopians, particularly

during a time when the promise of democratization was under threat.
